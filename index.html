Working Papers

Reevaluating Causal Estimation Methods with Data from a Product Release [paper]

Presented at IAAE 2026, COMPIE 2026. In submission.

[abstract]

Recent developments in causal machine learning methods have made it easier to estimate flexible relationships between confounders, treatments and outcomes, making unconfoundedness assumptions in causal analysis more palatable. How successful are these approaches in recovering ground truth baselines? In this paper we analyze a new data sample including an experimental rollout of a new feature at a large technology company and a simultaneous sample of users who endogenously opted into the feature. We find that recovering ground truth causal effects is feasible — but only with careful modeling choices. Our results build on the observational causal literature beginning with LaLonde (1986), offering best practices for more credible treatment effect estimation in modern, high-dimensional datasets.

Forecasting Algorithms for Causal Inference with Panel Data [paper]

Presented at NBER SI 2023, EC 2024. Submitted.

[abstract]

Conducting causal inference with panel data is a core challenge in social science research. We adapt a deep neural architecture for time series forecasting (the N-BEATS algorithm) to more accurately impute the counterfactual evolution of a treated unit had treatment not occurred. Across a range of settings, the resulting estimator (“SyNBEATS”) significantly outperforms commonly employed methods (synthetic controls, two-way fixed effects), and attains comparable or more accurate performance compared to recently proposed methods (synthetic difference-in-differences, matrix completion). An implementation of this estimator is available for public use. Our results highlight how advances in the forecasting literature can be harnessed to improve causal inference in panel data settings.

A Two-Step Procedure Using GANs for Panel Simulations

Draft available upon request

[abstract]

This paper introduces a two-step Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) approach for creating realistic synthetic panel data to evaluate causal panel estimators, extending prior work by Athey et al. (2024). While Monte Carlo simulations are the standard tool for assessing estimator performance, traditional simulation designs often rely on overly simplistic data-generating processes that fail to capture the complex structure of real-world panel data. We develop a novel approach that combines Wasserstein GANs with Long Short-Term Memory networks to learn and reproduce both cross-sectional heterogeneity and temporal dependencies inherent in panel data. Our two-step approach first implicitly learns the distribution of unit fixed effects, then learns the distribution of the remaining residuals conditional on the draw of the distribution in the first step. We apply this method to three canonical applications in the synthetic control literature: West Germany's economic performance, California's Proposition 99, and Basque terrorism. Our synthetic panels successfully pass both visual and statistical validation checks, despite the small sample sizes typical in these applications. Benchmarking multiple causal estimators on these realistic synthetic panels reveals promising results, though with moderate variation across datasets and estimators. These findings demonstrate that GAN-based approaches can effectively learn panel data distributions even with limited observations, offering researchers a more empirically grounded framework for evaluating the finite-sample properties of causal estimators in panel settings.
